DJS ARYA

#ImaginationDeployed

DJS Arya, the official canister satellite team of DJ Sanghvi College of Engineering (https://djsce.ac.in/), Mumbai, was formed in the year 2018 by a group of students from mechanical, computers and extc departments, intrigued by the world of space technology. Just as the name suggests, we build a fully functional miniature satellite integrated with space technology, which we present each year at the annual International Cansat competition , conducted by the American Astronomical Society (AAS) in collaboration with NASA. For the year 2021, our team is headed by our captain Satvik Deshmukh, our 2 Vice-Captains Aamir Khambaty and Vatsal Tapiawala as well as our team manager Rushabh Nagda.

About the CanSat competition

Held in Blacksburg, Virginia, it is a competition which allows students to get involved with the end-to-end life cycle of a complex engineering project, from conceptual design, through integration and test, the actual operation of the system and concluding with a post-mission summary and debrief. The CanSat competition typically consists of 3 rounds, namely the PDR round, which is presenting a review of the preliminary design, the CDR round, which is the critical design review and then finally, the launch of the CanSat - which takes place in the USA. An initiative that sparks the want to learn, the CanSat competition allows the curiosity in each student to grow, explore and venture into the world of space technology. Being a project-based competition it helps students understand concepts with a more realistic approach and helps them develop skills in various engineering aspects from programming to mechanics to electronics. Our team has not only had the honour of representing India at this scale but also bagged great ranks at both national and international levels.We find ourselves extremely privileged to be associated with this platform

What is a CanSat?

A CanSat is a simulation of a real satellite, integrated within the volume and shape of a soft drink can. It must typically consist of a container and a science payload. The challenge is to fit all the major subsystems found in a satellite, such as power, sensors and a communication system, into this minimal volume. The CanSat is then launched to an altitude of a few hundred metres by a rocket and helps us monitor altitude, airspeed and other such factors as well as help detect particulates in the air while gliding.
